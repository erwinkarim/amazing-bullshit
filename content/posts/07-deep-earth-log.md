Dr. Marcus Vance stared at the waveform on his monitor. For six months, the **Borehole-4 acoustic logs** had yielded nothing more than the low, monotonous groan of shifting tectonic plates two miles beneath the Siberian permafrost. It was tedious work, funded by a geological grant that was rapidly running out of patience.

It was 3:00 AM. Running on three hours of sleep and far too much espresso, Marcus applied a new experimental neural-net audio filter—a reckless tweak in noise cancellation meant to strip away ambient rock resonance.

The jagged, chaotic green lines on the screen didn't just clear up; they organized.

The tectonic groan melted away, replaced by something rhythmic, structured, and unmistakably artificial. A sequence of precise electronic tones, followed by a sudden frequency shift. Marcus held his breath, leaning closer to his monitor. He turned up the volume on his studio speakers and hit play.

---

Out of the speakers came a voice.

It was raspy, exhausted, and speaking in fragmented English, though warped by an accent that sounded centuries out of date.

> *"...if you can hear this, do not seal the upper hatch,"* the voice crackled through the static, fighting a wave of harsh white noise. *"They aren't trapped in the ice with us. We were trapped down here with them. And they just learned how to climb."*

The audio file cut abruptly to absolute silence.

Marcus sat frozen in the humming glow of his equipment, his skin prickling with sudden cold sweat. Before his brain could even begin to process the physics—let alone the implications—of a human voice broadcasting from two miles underground through solid rock, the heavy, soundproof steel door of his laboratory didn't just open.

It rattled violently from the *inside*, accompanied by the sound of wet, heavy footsteps echoing up the empty concrete maintenance stairs where no one else was supposed to be.
