Sol-Quarium

erwinkarim included in Sci-Fi
 2026-07-19  407 words   2 minutes 

The interface glowed with the soft, bioluminescent hum of a thousand stellar variables. With a gentle swipe of a three-jointed finger, the user nudged the slider. Atmospheric Hydrogen: +0.1%.

It wasn’t a game of direct control. You didn’t build cities or command armies; you simply whispered to the universe and watched the ripples.

A few millennia ago, a beautifully chaotic fluke had thrown the whole project off script. An errant cosmic ray—completely unprompted by the system—had sliced clean through the DNA of a bipedal primate. The resulting mutation made the creature absurdly, hypnotically charismatic. He started a major global religion, completely reshaping the geopolitical landscape of Orbit Three before his brief lifespan ended.

Lately, things had reached a fever pitch. The primate descendants were locked in a breathless, terrifying paradox. They were advancing and destroying themselves all at once.

Right now, on the main viewport, a digital trajectory projection flickered between two highly probable outcomes. One line showed a brilliant expansion, the species successfully colonizing their dusty gray moon. The other line was a violent, radioactive spike—a global nuclear war that would reset the board entirely. The tension was intoxicating.

“Xylar! Turn off the console, dinner’s ready!” A voice echoed from down the hall, vibrating through the floorboards.

“Two more minutes, Mom!” Xylar yelled back, his eyes glued to the screen as a missile test on Orbit Three flashed warning signs. “They’re about to launch!”

“No ’two more minutes,’ your father made Vrellex-looms and it’s getting cold! Shut the simulation down.”

Xylar sighed, a sound that translated across his species as a soft, whistling hiss. He couldn’t just leave it running on high temporal speed, or he’d come back to a scorched rock.

Reaching up, he pulled the physical override switch. The bioluminescent interface faded, replaced by a diagnostic status report. The words Simulation Mode: OFF didn’t appear. Instead, the console blinked with a network connection status:

EXTERNAL RELAY: ACTIVE QUANTUM LINK: SECURE PROBE ARRAY (SOL-OUTER BOUND): DEPLOYED

Xylar grabbed his jacket, giving one last look at the tiny, fragile blue marble spinning inside the containment field of the sub-space array. It wasn’t a simulation at all. It was just a very small, very real, very neglected corner of the local galaxy—manipulated by a few cloaked, microscopic probes parked right outside their Oort cloud.

“Coming!” Xylar called out, cutting the power and leaving eight billion tiny souls to figure out the next ten minutes on their own.
