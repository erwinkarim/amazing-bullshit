The Math Lesson

erwinkarim
 2026-07-22  447 words   3 minutes 

The dining room table was littered with crumpled papers, a half-empty mug of lukewarm coffee, and a third-grade math worksheet that might as well have been written in ancient hieroglyphics.

Leo stared at the problem for what felt like the hundredth time: If a train leaves Station A at 4:00 PM traveling at 45 miles per hour…

“I don’t get it,” Leo muttered, dropping his pencil with a dull clatter. “It doesn’t make any sense, Dad.”

Sitting across from him, his father leaned forward, adjusting his wire-rimmed glasses. He offered a warm, familiar smile—the kind that always made the room feel a little brighter.

“Take a breath, buddy,” his dad said, his voice smooth and patient. “You’re trying to solve the whole journey at once. Break it down. What do we know first?”

Leo sighed, rubbing his tired eyes. “We know the speed?”

“Right,” his dad encouraged, nodding gently. “And if it travels for two hours, what do we do with that speed?”

Leo looked at the numbers again. He traced the pencil lead over the paper, hesitant. “Multiply it?”

“Bingo,” his dad beamed, tapping the paper with his index finger. “See? You’re smarter than the problem, Leo. Write it down.”

Leo scribbled the equation. The numbers suddenly seemed to align, the fog clearing from his head. He did the final subtraction, double-checked his work, and gasped. “Ninety miles! The answer is ninety miles!”

“Look at that. You nailed it,” his dad beamed, a sound rich with pride. “I told you that brain of yours just needed a second to catch up.”

Leo grinned, reaching across the table to give him a high-five. But as his palm met his father’s hand, there was no solid touch—just the hum of a force-field hologram giving way under his weight, buzzing cold against his skin.

Before Leo could pull back, the dining room door clicked open. His mother stood in the doorway, her eyes red-rimmed and heavy with grief.

“Computer,” she said softly. “End dad sub-routine.”

The air crackled. The warm glow surrounding his father flickered wildly before shattering into a shower of blue particles that vanished into the ceiling vents. Just like that, the chair across from Leo was empty.

Only the homework remained, crisp and real on the wooden table, the pencil lead still dark where his father’s glowing fingers had guided it moments before.

His mother walked over, placing a trembling hand on Leo’s shoulder as he stared at the empty space, desperately holding back his tears.

“Leo, sweetheart, we can’t keep running these simulations to pretend he’s still here,” she whispered, her voice cracking. “I know it hurts, but we have to face the truth together.”
